# LOI Review — Letters of Intent

Help the user draft, review, or negotiate a Letter of Intent for a lease or purchase.

## When This Activates

- User says they want to make an offer
- User has received an LOI from a landlord/seller
- User asks for help drafting or reviewing an LOI
- User mentions "letter of intent" or "LOI"

## What an LOI Is (for non-RE users)

An LOI is a non-binding agreement that outlines the major deal terms before a formal lease or purchase contract is drafted. It's the opening handshake. Getting it right saves weeks of legal back-and-forth later.

**Important:** LOIs are typically non-binding (except for confidentiality and exclusivity clauses). But they set the negotiation anchor, so every term matters.

## Before You Draft or Review — Requirement Pre-Flight

Before drafting or reviewing an LOI, confirm these items are known. If any are missing, ask the user — these directly affect LOI terms and omitting them leads to costly lease amendments later:

- [ ] **Exact operations list:** What happens in the building? (machining, welding, assembly, chemical processing, testing, storage) — drives permitted use clause
- [ ] **Power requirements:** Voltage, phase, amperage, number of drops, backup power needs — drives electrical provision and TI scope
- [ ] **Chemical/hazmat use:** Any chemicals stored or used? (plating, coating, solvents, compressed gases) — drives permitted use, insurance, environmental, and fire suppression provisions
- [ ] **Heavy equipment:** Heaviest piece of equipment and its weight — drives floor load warranty and foundation requirements in TI
- [ ] **Ventilation/exhaust needs:** Any processes requiring fume extraction or dedicated exhaust? — drives TI scope and landlord approval
- [ ] **Loading requirements:** Number and type of dock doors, drive-in doors, truck court depth needed — drives landlord's work scope
- [ ] **Security/compliance:** ITAR, NIST, ISO, FDA, or other facility requirements? — drives access control provisions and landlord notice requirements
- [ ] **Expansion plans:** Will the tenant need more space within the lease term? — drives ROFO/ROFR and expansion option terms
- [ ] **Compressed air/specialty utilities:** CFM, PSI, number of drops — drives TI scope
- [ ] **Fire suppression requirements:** Does commodity type or chemical storage require upgraded suppression beyond standard ESFR? — drives landlord's work or TI

Cross-reference against the intake requirement document if one exists. Every item above should map to a specific LOI term.

## Dealbreaker Screen — Check These First

Before reviewing the full LOI, check these 5 terms. If any fails, stop and resolve it before spending time on the rest. These eliminate 80% of bad industrial deals:

1. **Permitted use covers your actual operations.** Does the LOI explicitly allow manufacturing, chemical processing, or whatever the tenant does? "Warehousing and distribution" kills most manufacturing deals. Cross-reference against municipal zoning — landlord consent alone isn't enough if zoning prohibits the use.
2. **Environmental liability is allocated.** Who owns pre-existing contamination? Is the landlord providing a Phase I ESA? If the LOI is silent on environmental, the tenant may inherit legacy contamination liability. Non-negotiable for any industrial user.
3. **Power infrastructure is sufficient or upgrade path is defined.** Does the building have the voltage, phase, and amperage the tenant needs? If not, does the LOI specify who pays for the upgrade, the timeline, and what happens if the utility can't deliver on schedule? Transformer upgrades: $50K-$200K, 6-12 months.
4. **TI allowance is realistic for industrial buildout.** Industrial TI runs $30-$80/SF for manufacturing (electrical, ventilation, foundations, chemical containment). If the landlord is offering $10/SF, the tenant is funding $600K+ out of pocket on a 30K SF space. Flag the gap.
5. **Timeline math works.** Add up: LOI negotiation (2-4 weeks) + lease negotiation (4-8 weeks) + permitting (4-12 weeks) + TI construction (12-36 weeks for manufacturing). If the tenant says "18 months," they may already be behind. Flag it.

If all 5 pass, proceed to the full review.

## LOI Review Checklist — Lease

If the user has an LOI to review, check for each of these terms:

### Essential Terms
- [ ] **Premises:** Address, suite/unit, exact SF (verify — landlords sometimes overstate)
- [ ] **Lease term:** Start date, length, commencement conditions
- [ ] **Base rent:** $/SF/yr or $/SF/mo? Triple Net (NNN), Modified Gross, or Full Service?
- [ ] **Rent escalations:** Annual bumps — fixed ($/SF) or percentage? CPI-based?
- [ ] **NNN estimates:** CAM, property tax, insurance — what are the actual numbers?
- [ ] **Free rent:** How many months? When do they apply (front-loaded or spread)?
- [ ] **TI allowance:** $/SF? What does it cover? Who manages the build-out?
- [ ] **Security deposit:** Amount? Letter of credit option?

### Terms People Miss
- [ ] **Renewal options:** How many? At what rate? How much notice required?
- [ ] **Expansion rights:** Right of first offer (ROFO) or right of first refusal (ROFR) on adjacent space?
- [ ] **Early termination:** Is there a kick-out clause? What's the penalty?
- [ ] **Assignment / sublease:** Can you sublease if your needs change?
- [ ] **Permitted use:** Is your specific use explicitly allowed?
- [ ] **Exclusivity period:** How long does the landlord hold the space for you?
- [ ] **Signage:** Building and monument signage rights?
- [ ] **Parking:** Ratio confirmed? Reserved spots?
- [ ] **After-hours access:** 24/7 or restricted?
- [ ] **Landlord's work:** What does the landlord build vs. what you build?

### Industrial Lease Provisions
For manufacturing, warehouse, or production tenants, these terms MUST be in the LOI — they are not optional:

- [ ] **Power/electrical:** Confirm voltage, phase, amperage, and transformer capacity are sufficient. If upgrades are needed, specify who pays (landlord vs. tenant), timeline, and whether landlord grants utility easement. CNC and heavy equipment may require 480V 3-phase, 800A+ service — inrush current on startup can be 5-8x running load.
- [ ] **Environmental provisions:** Who bears liability for pre-existing contamination? Require landlord to provide Phase I ESA. Allocate responsibility for tenant's own operations (chemical discharge, wastewater permitting). Manufacturing tenants should insist on environmental indemnification for conditions predating their occupancy.
- [ ] **Permitted use:** Must explicitly allow your specific operations — "manufacturing" is not enough if you do chemical processing (anodizing, plating, coating). Zoning must support the use AND the landlord's permitted use clause must not restrict it.
- [ ] **Ventilation/exhaust:** If processes require fume extraction, exhaust systems, or dedicated HVAC, specify in the LOI who installs and maintains these systems. Chemical processes (plating, coating) require OSHA/EPA-compliant ventilation — this is not a standard TI item.
- [ ] **Floor load and modifications:** If equipment exceeds standard floor load (CNC machines: 15,000+ lbs), specify landlord's warranty on slab thickness and tenant's right to install equipment pads/foundations without landlord approval delays.
- [ ] **Chemical storage and use:** If operations involve hazardous materials, the LOI must address: landlord's consent to chemical storage, quantities permitted, insurance requirements, and compliance with local fire code for chemical classification.
- [ ] **ITAR/security requirements:** Defense contractors need controlled access, visitor logging, secured storage. Specify tenant's right to install security infrastructure (access control, cameras, secured rooms) and that landlord access requires notice.
- [ ] **TI allowance specifics:** Industrial TI is more expensive than office TI. Specify what the allowance covers: electrical buildout ($15-$40/SF for heavy power), ventilation/exhaust ($15-$40/SF), chemical-resistant flooring ($8-$15/SF), equipment foundations ($3K-$10K per pad). If landlord manages buildout, require manufacturing-experienced GC.

### Red Flags — General
- Rent escalations above 3% annually (pushback — market standard is 2-3%)
- No TI allowance on second-gen space that needs work
- NNN estimates without a CAM reconciliation cap
- Personal guarantee requirement (especially for a company lease)
- Demolition clause (landlord can terminate for redevelopment)
- Relocation clause (landlord can move you to another space)

### Red Flags — Industrial Specific
Surface these proactively — industrial tenants lose real money on these:

- **No environmental indemnification for pre-existing conditions.** If the prior tenant contaminated the site, the new tenant can inherit liability. Remediation costs: $50K-$500K+. Always require landlord to indemnify for pre-lease environmental conditions and provide a Phase I ESA before signing.
- **Landlord approval rights on TI without timeline.** Industrial buildouts (electrical, ventilation, foundations) require landlord approval. If the LOI doesn't specify an approval timeline (10-15 business days max), landlords can delay months. For manufacturing tenants, every month of delay is lost production.
- **Power upgrade responsibility undefined.** Transformer upgrades cost $50K-$200K and take 6-12 months. If the LOI says "tenant responsible for all utility upgrades" without a cap or landlord contribution, the tenant eats the entire cost. Negotiate landlord participation or amortize into rent.
- **Insurance cost pass-through for manufacturing operations.** Landlords pass increased insurance costs to tenants via NNN. Chemical storage, welding, and manufacturing processes can increase building insurance 2-5x. Require the landlord to disclose current insurance costs and cap the tenant's share of increases caused by their operations.
- **Permitted use clause too narrow.** "Light manufacturing" may exclude chemical processing (plating, anodizing, coating). If the landlord's form LOI says "warehousing and distribution," your manufacturing use may be prohibited. Get the specific permitted use in writing.
- **Operating expense base year manipulation.** Landlords may set a base year with artificially low expenses, then reconcile to actual costs — surprise bills of $2-$5/SF. Require actual prior-year operating expense history.
- **Assignment/subletting restrictions.** Manufacturing facilities are expensive to build out. If you can't assign or sublease, you're trapped. Negotiate reasonable assignment rights — especially important for 7-10 year industrial leases.
- **Landlord's right to enter without adequate notice.** ITAR facilities require controlled access. Standard 24-hour notice clauses may violate security requirements. Negotiate 48-72 hour written notice with tenant escort requirement.

## LOI Review Checklist — Purchase

### Essential Terms
- [ ] **Purchase price:** Total and $/SF
- [ ] **Earnest money:** Amount, when due, hard vs. refundable
- [ ] **Due diligence period:** Length (30-60 days typical), what it covers
- [ ] **Financing contingency:** Included? How long?
- [ ] **Closing date:** Realistic given financing and diligence?
- [ ] **Title:** Warranty deed? What title exceptions are acceptable?
- [ ] **Survey:** Who pays? New or existing?
- [ ] **Environmental:** Who bears the risk? What if Phase II reveals contamination?

### Terms People Miss
- [ ] **Inspection rights:** Full access during diligence?
- [ ] **Existing leases:** Do they convey? At what terms?
- [ ] **Representations and warranties:** What does the seller rep about condition?
- [ ] **Prorations:** How are taxes, rents, and deposits prorated at closing?
- [ ] **Broker fees:** Who pays what? Is your representation accounted for?
- [ ] **Seller financing:** Available? What terms?
- [ ] **1031 exchange:** Is either party doing a 1031? Timing implications.

## Drafting an LOI

If the user needs to draft an LOI (not review one):

1. Confirm all deal terms from the checklist above
2. Draft in clear, simple language — no legalese
3. State that the LOI is non-binding except for confidentiality and exclusivity
4. Include a response deadline (5-7 business days is standard)
5. Present it to the user for review before they send it

### Industrial Provisions — Example LOI Language

For manufacturing/industrial tenants, include these provisions verbatim or adapted:

**Permitted Use:**
> "Tenant shall use the Premises for [specific operations, e.g., CNC machining, surface treatment including anodizing and plating, light assembly, warehousing, and ancillary office use], including the storage and use of chemicals and materials incidental to such operations, subject to compliance with applicable environmental and safety regulations."

**Environmental:**
> "Landlord shall provide a Phase I Environmental Site Assessment at Landlord's expense prior to lease execution. Landlord shall indemnify Tenant for any environmental contamination existing prior to the Commencement Date."

**Power/Electrical:**
> "Landlord shall deliver the Premises with [voltage]V [phase]-phase electrical service, minimum [amperage]A capacity. If upgrades to electrical infrastructure (including transformer) are required, Landlord shall [pay for / contribute $X toward / amortize over lease term at X%] such upgrades, to be completed no later than [date]."

**TI Approval Timeline:**
> "Landlord shall approve or provide written objections to Tenant's construction plans within fifteen (15) business days of submission. Failure to respond within such period shall constitute approval."

**Note:** An LOI is not a legal document. It does not replace an attorney. Always recommend the user have their attorney review the LOI and especially the subsequent lease or purchase contract.

## Negotiation Guidance

### Industrial LOI Benchmarks (use as starting points — adjust for local market)

| Term | Tenant-Favorable | Market Standard | Landlord-Favorable |
|---|---|---|---|
| Base rent escalation | 2%/yr fixed | 2.5-3%/yr fixed | CPI (uncapped) |
| Free rent | 1 month per lease year | 2-4 months on 5-7 yr | 0-1 months |
| TI allowance (industrial) | $30-$50/SF | $15-$25/SF | $5-$10/SF |
| TI allowance (mfg buildout) | $50-$80/SF | $30-$50/SF | $10-$20/SF |
| Renewal options | 2x 5yr at 95% FMV | 1x 5yr at FMV | None |
| Landlord TI approval | 10 business days | 15 business days | 30+ days / at discretion |
| Security deposit | 2 months | 3-4 months | 6+ months or LC |
| Assignment rights | Permitted with notice | Landlord consent (not unreasonably withheld) | Landlord sole discretion |
| Environmental indemnity | Full landlord indemnity + Phase I | Mutual indemnity post-occupancy | Tenant assumes all |

### Negotiation Tactics

- **Know the market.** Is this a landlord's market or a tenant's market? Vacancy rate drives leverage.
- **Lead with your strongest ask.** Don't save the big requests for later rounds.
- **Package concessions.** "We'll accept your rate if you give us 3 months free rent and a $15/SF TI allowance."
- **Always have a BATNA.** If you have another building, use it. "We're also considering [Property B]."
- **Don't negotiate against yourself.** Make an offer. Wait for their response. Don't preemptively concede.
- **Trade rent for TI.** Landlords often prefer higher rent over upfront TI dollars — offer a higher rate in exchange for a larger TI allowance. On a 7-year lease, $0.50/SF/yr in extra rent = ~$3.50/SF in TI at landlord's cost of capital.
- **Amortize infrastructure costs.** Transformer upgrades, ventilation systems, and other building improvements benefit the landlord after you leave. Negotiate to amortize these into rent rather than paying upfront.
