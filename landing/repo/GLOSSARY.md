# GLOSSARY — Industrial Real Estate Terms
> Plain English definitions for every term you'll encounter in an industrial deal.

---

## Lease Structure

**NNN / Triple Net**
You pay rent PLUS property taxes, insurance, and common area maintenance (CAM). The most common industrial lease structure. Add $1.50–$2.50/SF to quoted rent to estimate your true cost.

**Gross Lease**
Landlord pays operating expenses; you pay a flat rent. Rare in industrial. Usually means higher base rent.

**Modified Gross**
Hybrid — some expenses are included, some are passed through. Always ask which expenses are included.

**Base Rent**
The headline rent figure. Does not include NNN expenses.

**NNN Expenses / Operating Expenses / CAM**
Property taxes + building insurance + common area maintenance. In Houston: typically $1.50–$2.50/SF/year. Get the actual number from 12 months of operating statements — don't accept estimates.

**Expense Cap / Controllable Expense Cap**
A limit on how much NNN expenses can increase year-over-year. Typical negotiated cap: 5% annually on controllable expenses (excludes taxes and insurance).

**Escalation / Rent Bumps**
Annual rent increases built into the lease. Standard: 2.5–3.5% per year. This compounds — a 3% escalation means Year 5 rent is 16% higher than Year 1.

**Free Rent / Rent Abatement**
Period at lease start where you pay no rent. Market for new construction: 2–4 months. Existing buildings: 1–3 months. Negotiate this hard.

**TI / Tenant Improvement Allowance**
Money the landlord contributes toward build-out of your space. Expressed as $/SF. For vanilla industrial with office finish: $10–$25/SF. White box or warm shell: less. Full build-out: more.

**Warm Shell**
Building delivered with HVAC but otherwise unfinished. No office walls, no lighting in office area.

**As-Is**
Building delivered in current condition. Landlord makes no improvements. Negotiate TI allowance to offset.

**Personal Guarantee**
You personally guarantee lease obligations if your company can't pay. Landlords typically require 1–2 years of rent as personal guarantee. Negotiate: cap the amount, get a burndown (guarantee reduces over time).

**Holdover**
What happens if you stay past your lease expiration without a renewal. Most leases convert to month-to-month at 150–200% of final rent. Plan your timeline carefully.

## Building Specs

**Clear Height**
The usable vertical space from the floor to the lowest obstruction (usually sprinkler heads, HVAC, or structural members). Not the peak height. Not the eave height. The clear height. Verify with a laser measure — don't trust the listing.
- 18–22': Small bay, light industrial
- 24–28': Standard distribution and manufacturing
- 32–40': Modern big box logistics

**Dock-High Door**
Loading door raised above grade level (typically 48" high), accessed by truck trailers with a dock leveler. Standard: 8'W x 10'H or 9'W x 10'H. Count dock doors carefully — one per 10,000 SF is a common minimum.

**Dock Leveler**
The hydraulic or mechanical plate that bridges the gap between your dock floor and the trailer floor. Hydraulic = easier, more expensive. Mechanical = requires manual cranking. Condition matters.

**Grade-Level Door / Drive-In Door**
Door that opens at floor level — trucks or forklifts drive straight in. Critical for loading without a trailer, for drive-in operations, or for vehicle maintenance facilities.

**ESFR Sprinkler / Early Suppression Fast Response**
The gold standard for industrial fire suppression. Required for high-piled storage (racks over 12'). More expensive to install but preferred by most manufacturers and logistics users. If your operation involves racking, confirm ESFR coverage.

**Wet Pipe Sprinkler**
Standard sprinkler system — pipes always filled with water. Adequate for most uses. Not sufficient for high-piled storage without modifications.

**3-Phase 480V Power**
Standard industrial power. Required for most manufacturing equipment, heavy machinery, and high-capacity lighting. Verify amperage available — 800–1,200A is typical for mid-size industrial. Expansion capacity matters.

**Freestanding / Tilt-Wall / Metal Building**
- Tilt-wall: Concrete panels, most common modern industrial. Durable, good insulation.
- Metal/steel frame: Cheaper to build, common in Texas. Higher maintenance, more temperature variation. Harder to finance.
- Masonry: Older, often in urban infill locations.

**Bay Size / Bay Spacing**
Distance between structural columns. Wider bays = more usable floor space = better for racking and equipment. Standard modern: 50'x50' or 52'x50'. Older buildings: 30'x40' or tighter.

**Truck Court / Apron**
The paved area in front of dock doors where trucks maneuver. Minimum functional: 100'. Standard: 120–130'. Tight courts = accidents, slow operations, driver complaints.

**Cross-Dock**
Building with dock doors on both sides. Allows truck-to-truck transfer without warehouse storage. Common in distribution.

**Spec Building**
New construction built without a committed tenant. Developer is betting on the market to lease it. Often negotiable — developer wants to fill it.

**Build-to-Suit (BTS)**
Building designed and constructed specifically for your operation. Requires 12–24+ month lead time. Gives you exactly what you need. Landlord typically wants longer term lease (7–15 years) to justify the investment.

## Investment / Purchase Terms

**Cap Rate / Capitalization Rate**
Net Operating Income ÷ Purchase Price. The primary valuation metric for income-producing industrial. Higher cap rate = higher return = more risk or less desirable asset.
- Houston NNN credit tenant: 5.5–6.5%
- Houston value-add / older vintage: 7.0–8.5%
- National benchmark (stabilized): 5.0–6.5%

**NOI / Net Operating Income**
Gross rental income minus operating expenses. Does NOT deduct debt service. The number that drives cap rate valuation.

**NNN Sale-Leaseback**
Seller sells their building and simultaneously signs a lease to stay as a tenant. Unlocks capital from real estate while maintaining operational continuity. Common in manufacturing when a company needs growth capital.

**Pro Forma**
Financial projection for a property. Always ask what assumptions are baked in — vacancy, rent growth, expense growth. Conservative vs. aggressive pro formas are the difference between a good deal and a bad one.

**DSCR / Debt Service Coverage Ratio**
NOI ÷ Annual Debt Service. Lenders typically require 1.25x minimum. Means your income must be 25% more than your mortgage payment.

**Phase I / Phase II Environmental**
- Phase I: Review of historical use, visual inspection. Required for most commercial transactions. Looks for signs of contamination.
- Phase II: Actual soil/groundwater sampling. Done when Phase I finds a "Recognized Environmental Condition" (REC). Never waive Phase I rights.

**ALTA Survey**
Comprehensive land survey showing boundaries, easements, encroachments. Required by most lenders. Reveals issues that basic surveys miss.

## Texas-Specific Terms

**Freeport Exemption**
Texas property tax exemption for goods held in the state for less than 175 days and destined for export out of Texas. Major benefit for distribution companies — can eliminate property taxes on inventory.

**Chapter 380/381 Agreement**
City (380) or county (381) economic development agreements. The mechanism through which Texas municipalities offer property tax rebates and other incentives to attract businesses. See `INCENTIVES.md`.

**Texas Enterprise Fund (TEF)**
Governor's discretionary economic development fund. "Deal-closing fund" for large job-creating projects. Typically for 50+ job commitments.

**PACE Financing**
Property Assessed Clean Energy. Low-cost, long-term financing for energy efficiency improvements. Repaid through property tax bill. Can fund HVAC, lighting, insulation upgrades.
