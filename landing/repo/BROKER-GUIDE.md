# BROKER-GUIDE — How to Work With (and Without) an Industrial Broker
> The honest guide that brokers won't write themselves.

---

## The Truth About Industrial Brokerage

In a standard industrial deal, the **landlord pays the commission** — typically 4–6% of total lease value, split between the listing broker and the tenant rep broker. If you don't have your own broker, the listing broker often keeps both sides of the commission and represents both parties ("dual agency").

This means: **having your own broker costs you nothing and gets you someone whose job is to protect your interests.**

The catch: most tenant rep brokers are only motivated by deals that generate large commissions. Small deals (<5,000 SF, short terms) may get minimal attention from major firms. That's where this repo matters most — it gives you the tools to represent yourself effectively on deals where brokers aren't incentivized to fight for you.

## When You Need a Broker

**Use a broker when:**
- Deal size > 10,000 SF or > $500K total lease value
- You're purchasing (buyer's rep is standard)
- You're doing a sale-leaseback
- You're negotiating with an institutional landlord (REIT, private equity fund)
- You're in a market you don't know well
- The deal is complex (BTS, multi-phase, incentives negotiation)

**You can self-represent effectively when:**
- Deal size < 10,000 SF with a private landlord
- You're renewing an existing lease with a known landlord
- You have strong market knowledge and negotiating experience
- You've run this full workflow and know what you're doing

## How to Select a Tenant Rep Broker

Not all industrial brokers are equal. Most specialize in a specific size range, submarket, or deal type.

### The Interview Questions

Ask every broker you meet:

**Experience**
1. How many industrial deals have you closed in [my target submarket] in the last 12 months?
2. What is the typical size range of your deals? (Make sure it matches yours)
3. Have you represented tenants in [my industry]? What were the specific operational challenges?
4. Who are your last 3 tenant clients I can call for a reference?

**Market Knowledge**
5. What is the current vacancy rate in [my target submarket]? (If they can't answer quickly, move on)
6. What deals have closed in the last 6 months at my size and spec?
7. Where are the rent concessions right now — free rent, TI, or rate?
8. Which landlords in [submarket] are most motivated right now?

**Conflict of Interest**
9. Do you represent any landlords in the buildings you'll be showing me? (Dual agency is a conflict — understand it)
10. Does your firm have any exclusive listing agreements that might influence what you show me?
11. How are you compensated if I don't lease anything? (Answer: they're not — which is fine, just understand the incentive)

**Process**
12. How do you typically run a tenant search process?
13. How many options will you present before recommending one?
14. Will you attend tours with me or send an assistant?
15. Who will draft and negotiate the LOI — you or an attorney?

### Red Flags
- Can't quote current vacancy rates from memory
- Has listings in the same submarket and won't disclose conflicts
- Pushes you toward a deal quickly without letting you run a full process
- Hasn't closed a deal in your size range recently
- Focuses on their firm's brand rather than their personal deal volume

### The Shortlist
For Houston industrial: CBRE, JLL, Colliers, Transwestern, NAI Partners, and Finial Group all have strong industrial practices. Finial specializes in single-tenant, 10K–75K SF, owner-user deals — the segment this repo is built for.

For San Antonio: CBRE, JLL, NAI REOC, Colliers, and Finial Group (SA office) are active.

## How to Use This Repo WITH a Broker

**Don't hide it — share it.** A good broker will respect that you've done the work. A bad broker will be threatened by it. The latter tells you something.

**Use the repo to:**
- Come into the first meeting with a complete requirement summary (Workflow 01 output)
- Verify the broker's market claims against MARKET-CONTEXT.md
- Tour buildings with your own checklist (Workflow 05) instead of relying on the broker's judgment
- Know what questions to ask landlords (Workflow 06) before the broker filters what you learn
- Understand the deal terms you're negotiating (Workflow 07) without relying entirely on the broker's read

**Give the broker:**
- Your Requirement Summary
- Your target timeline
- Clear authority to make offers when you're aligned

## How to Use This Repo WITHOUT a Broker

**It's doable. Here's the honest assessment:**

You can run a complete search, tour buildings, and draft an LOI without a broker. Many companies do it.

What you'll miss:
- Broker-to-broker relationships (some deals are filled before they hit LoopNet)
- Institutional knowledge of specific landlords' motivations
- Commission leverage (you can sometimes negotiate the unleveraged commission as a credit to you)
- Experienced LOI and lease negotiation

**What to do if going without:**
1. Run Workflows 01–06 rigorously
2. Contact listing brokers directly and be clear you're self-represented
3. Hire a real estate attorney for the lease review — non-negotiable. Do not sign an industrial lease without a lawyer reviewing it. Budget $2,000–$5,000.
4. Use Workflow 07 to anchor your negotiations
5. Consider hiring a consultant broker for hourly advice rather than a full commission engagement

## The Commission Conversation

If you're self-representing and the landlord's broker has a 5% commission:
- That broker will earn 5% whether they represent one party or both
- You can negotiate: ask that the commission savings (the ~2.5% tenant side) be applied as additional TI, free rent, or rate reduction
- Some landlords will agree, some won't. It's worth asking.

If you have your own tenant rep broker:
- The commission is split 50/50 between listing and tenant rep (typically)
- You pay nothing directly — it comes from the landlord
- Your broker's interest is in closing a deal (any deal) — use this repo to make sure it's the RIGHT deal
