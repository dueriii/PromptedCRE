# INCENTIVES — Texas Industrial Location Incentives
> Read this before you commit to a location. Incentives can be worth $500K–$5M+ for manufacturing companies.
> ⚡ This file is updated as part of the autoresearch loop.

---

## The Rule

**Negotiate incentives before you sign anything.** Once you've committed to a location, your leverage disappears. The moment you execute a lease or purchase agreement, cities and counties know you're staying — and incentive conversations go cold.

Your sequence:
1. Identify 2–3 viable locations (different cities/counties if possible)
2. Contact economic development offices in each jurisdiction simultaneously
3. Let them compete for you
4. Only then commit

## Texas Incentive Programs

### 1. Chapter 381 / Chapter 380 Agreements (Most Common)
**What:** Property tax abatements and grants negotiated directly with counties (381) and cities (380).
**Value:** Typically 50–100% property tax abatement for 5–10 years. On a $5M industrial property, that's $50K–$150K per year in savings.
**Who qualifies:** Companies creating jobs and making capital investment. Manufacturing, distribution, and logistics all qualify.
**How to get it:** Contact the county economic development office (or city EDO) and say: "We're evaluating X county vs. [competitor county] for a [X job, $X investment] industrial facility. What incentives are available?"
**Timeline:** 60–120 days to negotiate. Do not start the process late.
**Texas counties with active Chapter 381 programs:** Harris, Bexar, Fort Bend, Montgomery, Guadalupe, Comal, Williamson

### 2. Texas Enterprise Fund (TEF)
**What:** Governor's "deal-closing fund" — direct cash grants for companies committing to large job creation.
**Value:** $1,000–$5,000 per job committed. $500K–$5M+ for qualifying projects.
**Who qualifies:** Typically 50+ jobs, $5M+ capital investment. Manufacturing and advanced industrial are priority sectors.
**How to get it:** Apply through the Governor's Office of Economic Development. Requires competing offer from another state.
**Website:** gov.texas.gov/business/page/texas-enterprise-fund

### 3. Skills Development Fund
**What:** Texas Workforce Commission funds customized job training through community colleges.
**Value:** Up to $500K per company for workforce training costs. Covers wages during training, curriculum development, equipment.
**Who qualifies:** Companies creating or retaining jobs in Texas. Manufacturing and technical trades are strong fit.
**How to get it:** Contact your local community college workforce division or Texas Workforce Commission.
**Why it matters for industrial:** If you're hiring machinists, welders, logistics coordinators, or any technical workforce — this pays for training your people.

### 4. Texas Freeport Exemption
**What:** Property tax exemption on goods, merchandise, and ores that are in Texas for 175 days or less and are destined to be shipped out of Texas.
**Value:** Can eliminate property taxes on your entire inventory. For high-inventory distribution operations, this is massive.
**Who qualifies:** Distributors, manufacturers, and logistics companies with products moving through Texas to out-of-state customers.
**How to claim:** File with your county appraisal district by April 30 of the tax year.
**Important:** Not all counties offer it. Harris County (Houston) and Bexar County (SA) both offer it. Confirm with the county appraisal district.

### 5. Manufacturing Sales Tax Exemptions
**What:** Texas exempts manufacturing machinery and equipment from sales and use tax.
**Value:** 8.25% on equipment purchases. On a $2M equipment investment, that's $165,000.
**Who qualifies:** Companies that manufacture tangible personal property for sale. Includes repair and replacement parts.
**Items covered:** Machinery directly used in production, pollution control equipment, material handling equipment used in manufacturing.
**How to claim:** Provide your vendor with a Texas Sales and Use Tax Exemption Certificate.

### 6. PACE Financing (Property Assessed Clean Energy)
**What:** Low-cost, long-term financing (10–30 years) for energy efficiency and renewable energy improvements. Repaid as a property tax assessment.
**Value:** Finance $100K–$10M+ in improvements at below-market rates. No upfront cost.
**Who benefits:** Companies paying high energy costs (manufacturing, cold storage, HVAC-intensive operations).
**What it can fund:** HVAC replacement, LED lighting, insulation, solar, compressed air systems, motors, chillers.
**Texas PACE:** C-PACE is available in most major Texas counties. Ask your landlord or lender about it — can be used for both owned and leased properties (with landlord consent).

### 7. Opportunity Zones
**What:** Federal program offering capital gains deferral and partial forgiveness for investments in designated census tracts.
**Value:** Defer capital gains taxes. After 10-year hold, eliminate gains on appreciation.
**Who benefits:** Investors purchasing industrial real estate in Opportunity Zones.
**Texas OZ locations:** Significant OZ coverage in parts of Houston (East End, Northside, some Ship Channel areas), San Antonio (East Side, South Side), and other metros.
**How to use:** Work with a Qualified Opportunity Zone Fund. Not for operating companies leasing space — relevant for purchases and development.

### 8. Foreign Trade Zone (FTZ) Benefits
**What:** Designated areas where goods can be imported, stored, processed, and re-exported without paying customs duties until entering U.S. commerce.
**Value:** Duty deferral, reduction, or elimination on imports. Significant for manufacturers importing components.
**Texas FTZ locations:** Houston (FTZ #84 — one of the largest in the US), San Antonio, Dallas, and others.
**Who benefits:** Manufacturers using imported components, distributors importing goods for re-export.
**How to access:** Your facility must be within or connected to an FTZ. Contact the Houston FTZ administrator or your freight forwarder.

## How to Have the Incentives Conversation

**Who to call:**
- County: Economic Development Corporation or County Judge's office
- City: City Economic Development Office or Mayor's Development Office
- State: Texas Economic Development & Tourism (Governor's office)
- EDC (Economic Development Corporation): Most major Texas counties and cities have one

**What to say:**
> "We're a [type of company] evaluating [City A] vs. [City B] for a [X SF] industrial facility. We expect to bring [X jobs] paying an average of $[X]/year and will invest approximately $[X] in equipment and improvements. We're on a [X month] timeline to make a decision. What incentive programs might be available to us, and who should we be talking to?"

**What they'll ask you:**
- Number of jobs (full-time, permanent)
- Average wage per job
- Capital investment (real property + equipment)
- Industry/NAICS code
- Timeline to occupancy
- Whether you have competing locations in other jurisdictions

**The leverage move:**
Always mention a competing location — even if you don't have a serious one. "We're also looking at [county adjacent to your target]" is often enough to get economic development staff engaged. They don't want to lose you to the next county over.

## Quick Reference by Situation

| Situation | Best Incentive to Pursue First |
|---|---|
| Buying/building a facility | Chapter 381/380 property tax abatement |
| High equipment investment ($1M+) | Manufacturing sales tax exemption |
| 50+ jobs, multi-state competition | Texas Enterprise Fund |
| High-inventory distribution | Freeport exemption |
| High energy costs | PACE financing |
| Importing components for manufacturing | FTZ status |
| Need to train new workforce | Skills Development Fund |
| Investor buying in targeted area | Opportunity Zone fund |

---

## The Timing Warning

**Chapter 381/380 agreements must be executed BEFORE you start construction or commit to the location.** Counties and cities cannot retroactively grant abatements on improvements already made. Start the incentives conversation at the same time you start the real estate search — not after you've signed.
